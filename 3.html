Notes on What Comes After

JoeBeau Nelson; A Death The Last Bear Hunt

1995

by Savannah "Savvy" Spears

I lost my dad at 9.

Fell right onto him.

husband lost his mom, I’ve lost step-parents, a brother, family, all of my childhood boyfriends and MOST of our old friends are dead. Drugs or suicide for almost all of these deaths. With this much around, it’s easy to understand that it’s either sink or swim…Or float, maybe. We float. We drift away on dark humor together. It’s only crass if anyone else knows, right?

There are many stories I can share about our (my husband and I) shared coping mechanism, but the most recent involves a Camaro Calendar. Chris found this old calendar in a field on a job site. He thought my daughter would like it - Gen Z and their love of retro. He brought it home and my daughter was showing me. My eyes honed in on 1995.

In 1995 my dad died in a trailer in Kentucky. He had just gotten out of prison for drug trafficking and it was my first time spending the night with him since his release. My mom had remarried. His lawyer. We were rich now and my 9-year-old self didn’t understand where I was, but I was dropped off with my stuffed bunny and my friend from Catholic school. She was more appalled than shocked, something even I picked up on, then. We watched movies and went to bed. I woke up and, given that I didn’t really know where I was - I didn’t really know where I was - so I wandered about the house and tripped over a camouflage comforter. My dad was in it. He’d died of a heart attack in the night. I don’t remember a lot about that night. What stood out was the “friend” who went back to school and told everyone about my dead dad in a trailer and then, suddenly, I had no friends.

Isn’t that weird? A 9-year-old bully stands out more? So, 1995 wasn’t great.

The calendar, though - they were figuring out what was happening in 1995. My dad died, I blurted out. The calendar was on September. “A few months before that month. Wait. After?.” Counts months on fingers, forgetting what month is when. My husband just starts laughing. I apologized. I didn’t know I was being weird. I was thinking about the “month trick” you do on your knuckles. Is May a knuckle? And then I laughed. What the hell was funny?


My dad had a Camaro. He was arrested in a Camaro. Cool calendar? Now we are all laughing.

Laughter is a normal reaction when you’re uncomfortable, but I think it was a paradoxical reaction. Both uncomfortableness and a realization of the small ironies in life.

You only lose someone once. Grief is a constant ebb and flow and it manifests in the strangest ways. I feel my dad’s death still. It left a deep scar on me, and even then, I laughed. I laugh every day, too. I'd say this is just life. It's weird, it's hard, it’s uncomfortable, and even makes you feel a little like a sociopath sometimes, but it's life, and for now, it's mine.

“The one who cries the loudest isn't the one who feels the deepest.”

I don’t know who said that, but the point is healing isn’t black and white. There’s no right or wrong. It’s been decades and I hope with every fiber of my being that when I go, my family has a good laugh about it, at some point. Not like, right then - there's such a thing as too soon, of course.

Humor is subjective. My family is discernibly objective, I guess. Living in an external reality where death is so close to home that it feels neighborly and therefore safe. I’m lucky to feel safe enough with my family to be authentic about my grief. I’m lucky I have them to laugh with - and I know my dad would’ve loved that calendar.
